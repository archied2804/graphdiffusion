# EXP-022: Finer diffusion schedule (T=1000)

> **One-line summary:** Quintuple the number of DDPM timesteps from T=200 to T=1000 (Ho et al.'s canonical choice). Hypothesis: the final reverse steps that clean high-frequency noise are under-resolved at T=200, producing the boundary wobble seen in EXP-020 row 1.

## Motivation

[[EXP-020_fourier_pressure_conditioning]] used T=200, which is unusually coarse — the original DDPM paper (Ho et al. 2020) and most follow-up work use T=1000. The last ~5–10% of reverse steps are where fine geometric detail emerges, and at T=200 there are only ~20 such steps. Spreading the same noise schedule over 5× more steps gives the score network more chances to "polish" the boundary in the low-noise regime, which is exactly where roughness manifests.

This experiment is contingent on [[EXP-021_long_train]] failing to close the gap on its own. If EXP-021 alone gets roughness below 0.024, EXP-022 becomes academic. If EXP-021 helps a little but doesn't pass the bar, EXP-022 builds on it (start from EXP-021's best config) and adds the diffusion-step lever.

## Hypothesis

- Boundary roughness drops further than EXP-021, ideally below 0.024.
- Training loss and val loss are *similar* to EXP-021 at convergence — Min-SNR-γ-style reweighting effects don't appear here because the loss is still uniform across t.
- Sampling time per batch grows ~5× (linear in T). Verify the interactive notebook still feels usable; if it doesn't, EXP-024 (EMA) plus EXP-025 (v-prediction) open the door to DDIM-style fast sampling later.

## Changes from parent (EXP-021)

Identical to [[EXP-021_long_train]] except `T=1000`. Same architecture, same dataset, same training budget. The cosine `beta` schedule is rebuilt internally at construction so β_start, β_end keep their meaning.

## Results

| Metric | EXP-020 | EXP-021 | EXP-022 | Δ vs EXP-021 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Best val loss | 0.00306 (ep 59) | 0.00010 (ep 986) | **0.00009** (ep 988) | **−10%** |
| Boundary roughness (overall) | 0.0338 | 0.02125 | **0.01523** | **−28%** ✅ |
| Boundary roughness (train #0) | 0.0125 | 0.01547 | **0.00991** | −36% |
| Boundary roughness (train #1) | 0.0759 | 0.02629 | **0.00625** | **−76%** |
| Boundary roughness (train #2) | 0.0174 | 0.01029 | **0.00873** | −15% |
| Boundary roughness (synth asym.) | 0.0293 | 0.03296 | 0.03604 | +9% |
| Sample wall-clock (CFG=3, n=4) | ~1.5 s | ~1.5 s | ~7.5 s (5×) | — |

### Observations

- **T=1000 compounds EXP-021's win.** Overall roughness drops a further 28% on top of EXP-021 (which itself was a 37% drop from EXP-020). The three "clean" training rows improve substantially; train #1 specifically dropped 76% to 0.00625, an order of magnitude below EXP-020's 0.0759.
- **The synthetic OOD target regressed slightly** (+9% vs EXP-021). The extra denoising steps don't help where the conditioning is already out-of-distribution; if anything they let the model commit more confidently to a wobbly OOD shape. Worth re-checking with `--target-seed` variations.
- **Val loss is essentially flat** between EXP-021 (0.00010) and EXP-022 (0.00009). The roughness win isn't visible in MSE — confirms that the smoothing is happening in the *sampling* stage (more reverse steps = more polish), not in better gradients during training.
- **Sampling cost grew 5×.** ~7.5 s per 4-shape batch on this GPU. The interactive notebook is still usable at this speed (drag-release feel is similar to before), so no need to fall back to a fast DDIM sampler yet.

## Conclusions

- **New best checkpoint.** EXP-022 supersedes EXP-021 as the recommended pointer for the interactive notebook.
- Hypothesis confirmed: the boundary roughness was bottlenecked by the *number of reverse-process steps*, not by training convergence. EXP-021 closed the convergence gap; EXP-022 closes the sampling-resolution gap.
- The remaining experiments (EXP-023 Min-SNR-γ, EXP-024 EMA, EXP-025 v-pred) still run as planned — they may compose with T=1000.
